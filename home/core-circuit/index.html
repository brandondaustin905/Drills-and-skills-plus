The one rule for the whole circuit

Your lower back stays pressed to the floor in every face-up exercise.

If there's a gap under your back, you're not in a hollow position — you're just lying down with your legs up. That single check governs the hollow hold, the dead bug and the pike snap-up.

1
20 seconds

Hollow hold

Lie flat. Arms overhead by your ears, legs straight and together. Lift shoulders and legs slightly off the floor.

What to feelDeep abdominal engagement · ribs pulled down · the whole body working as one unit
Errors & fixesBack arches → press the belly button down harder, or raise the legs · Bent knees → extend and squeeze the thighs · Neck strain → eyes slightly up, don't jam the chin
Beginner
Tuck the knees into a tuck hollow.
Advanced
Lower the legs closer to the floor without losing back contact.
2
20 seconds

Superman hold

Face down. Arms extended forward, legs straight together. Lift arms, chest and legs slightly. Squeeze glutes and upper back.

What to feelGlutes, lower back and shoulders working · a long, strong body line
Errors & fixesOver-arching the low back → lift from the glutes, don't collapse the spine · Floppy legs → squeeze the thighs · Arms high or uneven → keep them long and reaching
Beginner
Lift only the upper body, or only the legs.
Advanced
Small pulsing lifts while holding.
3
15 reps

Dead bugs

On your back, arms straight up, knees bent at 90° in tabletop. Press the lower back down. Slowly extend the opposite arm and leg, then return. Alternate for 15 total.

What to feelDeep core stability · control through hips and ribs · zero movement in the lower back
Errors & fixesBack lifting → slow down, reduce the range · Fast sloppy reps → move deliberately · Losing coordination → reset between reps
Start here if the hollow is too hardThe dead bug teaches the same lower-back control with far less load. Build it here, then progress to the tuck hollow, then the full hollow.
4
10 reps

Pike snap-ups

Flat on your back, arms overhead, legs straight together. Snap arms and legs up simultaneously into a tight V. Control the way back down slowly.

What to feelExplosive core contraction · a fast snap from hollow to tight
Errors & fixesUsing momentum → slow the reset phase · Bent knees → lock the legs · Weak snap → squeeze the abs hard at the peak
Beginner
Tuck snap-ups, knees bent.
Advanced
Pause one second at the top of the V.
5
20 seconds each side

Side plank

Elbow under shoulder. Legs straight, or stacked for balance. Lift the hips into a straight line, body aligned head to feet.

What to feelObliques working · hips lifted and stable · a straight, rigid line
Errors & fixesHips dropping → squeeze the glutes and lift higher · Shoulder collapsing → push the floor away · Twisting → stack the shoulders and hips
Beginner
Bottom knee on the floor.
Advanced
Top leg lifted, or small hip dips.

Breathe

Holding your breath is the most common mistake in every hold on this page. It makes you rigid, burns you out early, and it's why athletes fail at 15 seconds when they could hold 25.

The testSteady breathing throughout. You should be able to talk while you hold.

Why this matters

This isn't core work. It's performance control training.

Every clean jump needs a strong hollow position. Every stunt needs a stable midline. Every tumbling pass needs controlled body tension. Every landing needs core absorption.

Weak core: jumps break early, landings get heavy, stunts wobble, tumbling loses shape.

Strong core: you look tighter in the air, land quieter and safer, hold positions longer, perform with confidence under fatigue.

That's the difference between athletes who try hard and athletes who look clean under pressure. Own every second. Own every rep.
