Why this one matters more than it looks

Most things sold as injury prevention have thin evidence behind them. This doesn't.

Programs that teach athletes to land properly — bent knees, controlled, knees tracking over toes — are among the best-evidenced interventions in youth sport for reducing knee injury risk, particularly ACL injuries in female athletes.

↑More knee bend on landing
↓Less inward knee collapse

So this isn't just a scoring drill. Done properly and consistently, it changes how your knees handle every landing you'll ever do.

Where that comes from

We're specific about sources on claims this big.

The review Influence of Neuromuscular Training Interventions on Jump-Landing Biomechanics and Implications for ACL Injuries in Youth Females: A Systematic Review and Meta-analysis — published in Sports Medicine, April 2025.
What it found Training interventions aimed at reducing ACL injury risk in young females significantly increased peak knee flexion angle and reduced knee valgus motion compared with control groups. Those are the two things this drill trains directly.
A study it cites Six weeks of plyometric training in 15-year-old female athletes reduced landing force from a vertical jump by 1.2 times bodyweight. Six weeks.
The honest caveat The same review found that effects on other measurements were non-significant or small. So: the evidence is strong for the specific things this drill targets — knee bend and knee collapse — and weaker elsewhere. That's still a much better foundation than most training advice has, and it's exactly why this page focuses on those two things and doesn't overclaim beyond them.

The thing your knees must not do

The dangerous pattern is called knee valgus — dynamic knee collapse. The hip rotates inward, the knee caves toward the midline, the ankle rolls in. That combination is the mechanism behind most non-contact ACL injuries.

The fix is your glutes. Strong glutes resist the inward collapse — which is why "push the knees slightly outward and squeeze" is the correct cue, not just a coaching preference.

Worth knowing: before puberty, boys and girls land essentially the same way. After puberty, female athletes tend to develop the patterns that raise ACL risk. There's a window — the earlier you train good landings, the more they stick.

The rep

3 sets of 10. Rest 30–45 seconds between sets. Every rep follows the same four beats.

1
Up, not out

Jump straight up

Dip slightly — controlled, not deep. Swing your arms up fast. Jump vertically; don't travel forward.

The cueUp, not out. If you're drifting forward across the floor, the dip is too deep or you're leaning.
2
In the air

Stay tight

Body tight, core engaged, legs straight. Prepare for the landing before you hit the ground — a landing you didn't plan for is a landing you absorb badly.

3
The whole point

Stick it

Land and freeze. Hold two seconds, every single rep.

Knees
Track over the toes. Never caving inward.
Chest
Stays lifted.
Core
Tight.
Feet
Flat and quiet.
Steps
None. Not one.
4
Don't skip

Reset

Stand tall again before the next jump. Don't rush — the reset is what makes each rep a separate, deliberate rep rather than a bounce.

Mistakes & fixes

Landing loud or heavyBend the knees softly. → Don't lock your legs on impact.
Knees collapsing inwardThe important one. → Push the knees slightly outward and squeeze your glutes.
Falling forward or backwardChest lifted. → Weight centred over the middle of your foot.
Rushing repsReset fully between each jump. → Quality over speed, always.

Adjust it

Beginner

Smaller jump height. Practise just jump → land → hold, with no focus on speed. Step down instead of jumping if you need to.

Advanced

Increase jump height while keeping the landings silent. Hold each landing 3–5 seconds. Add fatigue by running this after another drill or circuit.

The honest testIf height goes up but the landings get louder, you've gone backwards. Silence is the standard, not height.

Why this matters

In cheerleading a landing is never just a landing. It's where routines are scored, stabilised, and remembered.

A clean landing shows control, confidence and safety. A messy landing shows instability — even if the skill in the air looked strong.

And unlike almost anything else you'll practise, this one protects you for the rest of your career.
