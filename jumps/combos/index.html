Combos

The transition isn't the gap between skills. It's the skill judges are scoring.

⏱ 6 min read🔗 Connection focus🏆 Routine-level

The one idea this turns on

Judges don't score your jumps and your transitions separately. They see one continuous thing.

So the moment between two jumps isn't a rest — it's a scored part of the skill. Most athletes train the jumps and ignore the connective tissue, then wonder why their combo scores lower than their individual jumps.

The transition is the skill. Everything below follows from that.

Where combos are won and lost

Training skills one at a time doesn't prepare you to do them in a row, under fatigue, with the timing of one bleeding into the next. Combos train the switch itself: staying clean while moving, not just while standing still.

The worked example

Toe touch → pike → hurdler. Three jumps, two transitions — and the transitions are where the lesson lives.

The jumps themselves you already have from the technique lesson: toe touch wants width and lift, pike wants straight legs and a fast hip fold, hurdler adds asymmetry so both sides need working evenly. What's new is what happens between them.

Transition 1 · toe touch → pike

Land the toe touch balanced, then instantly: posture tall, core re-tightened, arms already moving toward the pike prep. No pause to gather. The landing of jump one is the prep for jump two.

Transition 2 · pike → hurdler

Same again — stable base, eyes forward, breathe, posture corrected, all in a fraction of a second. This is the one that breaks under fatigue, because it's last.

The cue for bothLand loaded, not landed. You're arriving into the next jump, not finishing the previous one.

How to actually build a combo

The order is fixed. Skip a stage and you reinforce whatever's broken, faster.

1 · Clean. Each jump alone, slow, perfect. A messy single jump has no chance in a combo.

2 · Connected slow. Link them at half speed. A full stop-and-reset between jumps is fine here — you're learning the sequence, not the tempo.

3 · Controlled. Shrink the pause. The reset gets faster and tighter but stays deliberate. The transition becomes a skill instead of a gap.

4 · Full speed. Only now. Real tempo, landing loaded into each next jump.

5 · Performance. Under fatigue, on count, synced with the team, arms added between jumps.

Why speed comes last

Accuracy is built before speed — one of the most consistent findings in how people learn movement.

Rush a combo early and you don't just get a messy rep, you train the messy rep. The sloppy landing, the slow reset, the dropped chest all get wired in, and then you spend months unlearning them.

The tradeSlow reps feel less impressive and produce a better combo. Every time.

The three things that break combos

Treating each jump as separateThe root of all the others. "Jump, then jump, then jump" instead of one connected phrase — and every transition gets neglected.
Landings you check out ofEvery landing is a setup. A landing you've mentally left is a bad prep for the next jump.
Fatigue collapseChest drops, posture goes, reset slows — and it hits the last jump hardest. Which is exactly why you train combos tired on purpose.

Training it tired — on purpose

Once a combo is clean and fast when you're fresh, the last step is making it survive when you're not.

Do your combos after a conditioning circuit or a hard section, when the legs are heavy and the reset wants to get lazy. That's competition condition. A combo that only looks good fresh isn't ready.

The lineFatigue is the training tool. Form breakdown is the stop signal. When the shape genuinely falls apart, the set is done — you're now training the collapse.

Where this shows up

A clean combo isn't just a jump section. The same skill — moving between elements without stopping — carries into dance transitions, tumbling setups and stunt timing.

Athletes who own integration look more polished for one specific reason: their movement never visibly stops. That continuous quality is a big part of what reads as elite from the floor.

Safety

Warm up first — combos are more demanding than single jumps.

Land soft every rep. Repeated hard landings add up fast on knees and ankles.

Don't grind endless jumps without recovery. Quality over count, always.

Stop when form breaks, not when the reps run out. Controlled fatigue is useful; lost technique is just risk.

For growing athletes, keep combo volume modest and take knee or heel pain seriously.

For parents and coaches

Combos look simple from outside and are one of the most demanding things in cheer — strength, flexibility, timing, coordination, endurance and focus, all at once, in sequence.

The most useful thing to reinforce: slow down and fix the transitions. Athletes want to speed up and add difficulty; the ones who get the connections clean first end up further ahead. Watch the moment between the jumps.
