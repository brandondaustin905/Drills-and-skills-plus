Why your splits don't equal your jump

There are two kinds of flexibility, and only one shows up in a jump.

Passive range is how far your leg goes when something else moves it — gravity, a band, the floor. That's what a straddle stretch measures.

Active range is how far you lift it yourself, unassisted, against gravity. That's what a toe touch is.

This is why athletes with flat splits still have low jumps. The range exists. The strength to reach it under their own power doesn't.

Different jumps, different limiters

Generic stretching gets generic results. Know what you're actually training for.

Find your limiter

Four tests. Do them once, then again in six weeks. Whichever fails worst is what your training should prioritise.

1 · Hip flexorsLie on your back at the edge of a bed or bench. Pull one knee to your chest and let the other leg hang off the edge.Hanging thigh floats up instead of resting level? Tight hip flexors on that side.
2 · HamstringsLie flat. Lift one straight leg as high as it goes while the other stays down.Roughly 80–90° is workable. Well under that, hamstrings are your limiter.
3 · AdductorsSit in your straddle with kneecaps pointing straight up. How wide before they start rolling in?That's your honest number — not how wide you can force it.
4 · The active gapFrom test 2, lower the leg to the floor. Now lift it back to that same height with nothing helping.The difference between the two heights is your active gap. Most athletes have never measured this, and it's usually the biggest number on the page.

How range actually improves

Progress comes largely from teaching your nervous system that a new position is safe — raising tolerance and quieting the protective reflex. It isn't simply pulling fibres longer.

That reframes everything. Forcing a stretch signals danger, and the body tightens against you. Consistency and calm breathing signal the opposite.

Which is whyNo bouncing, ever. Tension, never pain. 20–30 seconds minimum, up to 45 on deep positions. Core braced — dumping into the lower back to fake range isn't flexibility, it's a habit that becomes back pain.
Timing, plus shoulder and spine range, are in Tumbling+ · Flexibility & Range. Short version: dynamic before you train, static after.

If you're mid-growth-spurt

During a growth spurt, bones lengthen before muscles and tendons catch up. Flexibility often gets temporarily worse, and it's common to feel tighter and more awkward than you did six months ago.

That's normal. It's temporary. It isn't a sign you've been lazy, and it isn't a reason to force harder — forcing during this window is exactly when things get hurt.

What matters: keep the work consistent and gentle, don't chase the range you used to have, and take knee or heel pain seriously rather than pushing through. Growing athletes are more prone to both.

Warm up first

Never stretch cold. Cold tissue resists and fights back.

Jumping jacks × 50High knees, 30sWalking lunges × 10 eachLeg swings × 10 each way

Leg swings loose and pendulum-like, wall for balance. Chest high through the lunges.

2
Toe touch lift

Hip opener lunge

Tight hip flexors physically pull your legs back down during a toe touch. This is the one that most directly costs you height.

Kneel on your right knee, left foot flat in front, both legs around 90°. Tuck the tailbone under and squeeze the right glute hard. Shift hips gently forward, chest completely upright. Right arm reaches to the ceiling.

3
Pike & hurdler

Banded hamstring stretch

Lie flat, band around the arch of the right foot, left leg flat with toes to the ceiling. Pull the right leg slowly toward your chest, knee locked.

Squeeze the quad hard while you hold. Engaging the front of the thigh helps the hamstring let go.

CheckKnee bends means you've pulled too far. Lower until the leg is perfectly straight — a bent-knee version is a different, easier stretch.
4
Toe touch width

Straddle

Standing: feet wide, toes forward, hinge with a flat back, hands to the floor, walk them back in line with the heels. Weight slightly forward, not sitting into the heels.

Seated: legs as wide as they'll go, sit tall, walk the hands forward slowly.

5
Posterior chain

Pike

Legs zipped tightly together. Flex the feet hard, toes pulling back toward the shins — that brings the calves in alongside the hamstrings.

Reach both arms up to lengthen the spine, then hinge from the hips reaching past the toes. Ankles or shins if the toes are out of reach — never bend the knees.

The upgrade: contract-relax

Once the basic holds feel easy, this gets more from the same time. Use it on the hamstring and straddle stretches.

Two cautionsNever with a partner pushing you — the pressure comes from you, into something immovable, so you control it entirely. And skip it completely if anything hurts.
6
The part that counts

Close the active gap

Straight after the stretches, while the range is fresh. This is what turns floor flexibility into jump height, and it's the part everyone skips.

Straddle lifts. Sit in your straddle, hands on the floor, lift both legs a few inches under their own power. Hold 3 seconds. 8 reps.

Pike lifts. Sit in a pike, hands beside your hips, lift both straight legs off the floor. Hold 3 seconds. 8 reps.

Side leg lifts. Standing or lying, lift the straight leg toward toe-touch height with nothing helping. Hold 3 seconds at the top. 8 per side.

Expect thisIf these feel far harder than the stretches, that's your gap — and it's the honest measure of what your jump can currently use.

What a week looks like

3× / week
Full session, 25 min. On days you're not doing your heaviest training, or after it. Never immediately before jumping.
Other days
Active work only, 5 min. The lifts are low-fatigue and respond well to frequency.
Pre-practice
Dynamic only — leg swings, lunges, high knees. No long holds; they can temporarily blunt power.

Missing sessions costs you more than any single session gains you. Three short weeks beat one heroic one.

What progress actually looks like

"I've stretched for months and nothing's changed"

Work through these in order.

Are you doing active work?All passive stretching means your jump has no way to use the range. → Most common cause by a wide margin.
Are you consistent?Three times a week for eight weeks beats daily for two weeks then nothing. → Frequency over intensity.
Are you forcing it?Pain and bouncing make the body defensive. → Backing off often produces faster progress.
Are you holding long enough?Ten seconds isn't a stretch. → Twenty minimum.
Are you growing?Range often goes backwards mid-spurt. → Holding steady is the win right now.
Is it structural?Some limits are joint shape, not muscle tightness, and stretching won't change those. → If one position has never moved despite all of the above, ask a physiotherapist rather than pushing harder.

Safety

Never bounce, never force, no partner pushing you deeper. Range built by force comes off just as fast and takes something with it.

Tension, never pain. Sharp pinching in a hip, or anything down the back of the leg, is a stop-and-report.

More range isn't automatically better. Range you can't control with strength is instability, not flexibility.

Growing athletes: gentle and consistent, not aggressive. Knee or heel pain gets looked at, not pushed through.
