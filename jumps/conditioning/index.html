Conditioning is not punishment

Worth saying plainly, because it gets used that way in a lot of gyms.

Athletes who learn that push-ups mean they did something wrong will train them badly for their whole career. Every exercise here has a purpose — and knowing the purpose is what makes an athlete do it properly.

Flexibility is in Mobility for Jumps. Execution and scoring are in What Judges Actually See.

Why technique dies late in a routine

Early on, explosive skills run on the ATP-PC system — immediate energy, good for bursts of roughly 10 seconds. As the routine continues the anaerobic system takes over: energy fast, but by-products accumulate and produce the familiar burn.

Then the part that actually costs you points. As fatigue builds, your nervous system gets less efficient at activating muscle fibres. That isn't weakness — it's a signalling problem — and it shows up as lower jumps, bent knees, slower motions, lost body tension, poor timing and worse balance.

Power vs fatigue resistance

Two different goals, two different methods. Mixing them gets you neither.

Do bothPower work fresh, conditioning after — different days, or different parts of a session. Never the other way round.

Warm up first

Never condition cold. 5–10 minutes of dynamic movement: light jogging, jumping jacks, high knees, butt kicks, leg swings, arm circles, bodyweight squats, walking lunges.

Dynamic work prepares the body for explosive movement far better than static stretching does.

The ruleMove with intention. Quality before speed, every single round.
1
Power

Squat jumps

Plyometric work using the stretch-shortening cycle: lowering stores elastic energy, exploding upward releases it — producing more force than a jump without the loading phase.

Stand shoulder-width. Brace. Lower into a squat with the chest lifted. Drive up through the heels to full extension. Land softly with bent knees.

Watch forKnees collapsing inward · landing on locked knees · leaning too far forward · losing balance
2
Skill under fatigue

Standing toe touches

Repeating correct movement patterns strengthens the connection between brain and muscle. Practising technically correct toe touches while slightly tired is what makes them hold up at 2:15.

Chest tall. Snap the arms. Straight knees, pointed toes, soft landing.

Stop the setIf your form breaks, the set is over. A sloppy toe touch trains a sloppy toe touch.
3
Stability

Plank hold

Your core isn't just abs — it's the abdominals, obliques, deep spinal muscles and glutes working together to transfer force between upper and lower body. Without that, power made by the legs is lost before it reaches the arms.

Elbows under shoulders. Straight line head to heels. Glutes tight, core tight, breathing steady.

4
Hip speed

Pike snap-ups

Trains rapid hip flexion. Your hip flexors drive pike jumps, toe touches and standing tumbling — the faster they contract, the faster your legs arrive in position.

5
Ankles

Calf raises

Your calves generate the final push in every jump and help absorb landing force.

Stand tall. Rise onto the toes. Pause briefly. Lower slowly.

6
Endurance

Wall sit

Routines are full of held partial-squat positions — transitions, catching stunts, prepping jumps. This builds the ability to sustain force over time.

Knees at roughly 90°. Back flat to the wall. Weight even. Breathe normally.

Progressing it

The circuit stops working once it's easy. Progress in this order:

Weeks 1–2
2 rounds, 90 seconds rest. Get the technique right.
Weeks 3–4
3 rounds, 90 seconds rest.
Weeks 5–6
3 rounds, 60 seconds rest.
After that
Add reps, or add a round.
Order mattersShorten the rest before adding reps. Shorter rest is what builds fatigue resistance specifically.

The most sport-specific conditioning there is

Nothing in a gym conditions you for a routine like the routine does.

Once the circuit is comfortable, add section run-throughs full-out — jump section, then tumbling section, then a stunt sequence, with realistic short breaks between.

That reproduces the actual demand: explosive effort, minimal recovery, repeat. Circuits build the base. Routine work is where it transfers.

Recovery is where improvement happens

Conditioning creates small amounts of stress in the muscle. Repair is what makes it stronger — and repair happens between sessions, not during them.

Drink enough water. Eat enough, including protein and carbohydrates. Sleep 8–10 hours; teenage athletes genuinely need the upper end. Take rest days. Light stretching after training.

Worth internalisingTraining every day isn't dedication. It's interrupting the part that makes you better.

Safety

Warm up first, always. Supportive athletic shoes.

Fatigue is expected. Pain is not. Stop for sharp pain, dizziness, or anything unusual.

Stop the set when technique breaks, not when the reps run out.

2–3 sessions a week. Not daily — muscles need recovery time to adapt.

For growing athletes, keep jumping volume modest and take knee or heel pain seriously rather than pushing through it.

For parents and coaches

Athletes think conditioning means as many reps as possible. It doesn't — it means building movement patterns that hold up when it counts.

Praise technique and consistency rather than rep counts or finish times. And never use conditioning as punishment — athletes who associate it with failure will avoid the thing that makes them better.
