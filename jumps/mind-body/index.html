What this lesson is — and isn't

The Tumbling+ mental lesson is about fear: blocks, balking, getting a skill back when your body refuses.

This one is about learning: how skills get built in the first place, and how you make them repeat identically under pressure.

Different problems. Different tools.

Every skill starts in the brain

Practise a movement repeatedly and your brain strengthens the pathways responsible for it. That's motor learning.

Each correct rep makes your nervous system better at recruiting the right muscles, timing when they fire, coordinating the pattern, and cutting wasted tension.

People call it muscle memory — but the muscles aren't remembering anything. The brain and nervous system are the ones adapting.

The catchCorrect repetition builds the pattern. Incorrect repetition builds one too — just the wrong one. Twenty rushed toe touches don't do nothing; they actively train a rushed toe touch.
Tool 1

Visualization

Rehearsing a skill mentally, without moving. Doing it activates many of the same pathways your brain uses in actual performance.

How to do it properly

Eyes closed. Run the skill start to finish at real speed — no fast-forwarding through the hard part.

Focus on sharp arm placement, full extension, clean lines, a soft controlled landing.

And critically: imagine how it feels, not just how it looks. Imagining the sensation of a movement resembles real execution more closely than picturing it from the outside.

Dose5–10 minutes, a few times a week. Consistency beats one long session.
What it won't doReplace physical reps. Visualization builds familiarity and sharpens patterns you already have. It doesn't install new ones from scratch.
Tool 2

Slow-motion reps

Breaking a skill into phases and performing each deliberately, at a fraction of normal speed.

Why it works: when you rush, your brain skips the small corrections. Slowing down forces attention onto every detail — improving awareness, coordination, timing, and how well the technique sticks.

Dose5–8 slow reps. This is precision work, not conditioning.
Tool 3

Breathing

The rule that matters: don't hold your breath.

Athletes under pressure hold their breath without noticing. It creates tension, burns energy and makes movement rigid — and it's a major reason technique degrades late in a routine.

A workable pattern for jumps: exhale through the lift, inhale on the landing and reset. Breathing out as you exert helps engage the deep core muscles that stabilise your trunk and transfer force between lower and upper body.

Don't overthink itBreathing continuously and rhythmically matters far more than any specific count.

Putting it together

Athletes who perform identically under pressure train four things, not one:

Physical repetition · Mental rehearsal · Controlled execution · Breath awareness

That combination is what lets a skill hold up in practice, in a routine section, under fatigue, and on competition day.

Common mistakes

Rushing through visualization. Treating mental training as optional. Doing slow reps without actually focusing. Holding your breath. Assuming physical reps are the only thing that counts. Skipping mental rehearsal on tired days — which are exactly the days it's most useful.

The real oneMental training only works when it's done with intention. Going through the motions of going through the motions does nothing.

When to use it

Three to five times a week, 5–10 minutes.

Before practice
Visualization as a warm-up.
After practice
Slow-motion corrections while the skill is fresh.
Rest days
Mental rehearsal only, no load.
Before comp
Focus and confidence.

Rest days are the underrated one. You can rehearse patterns without adding physical stress — genuinely useful when you're managing volume or working around an injury.

For parents and coaches

Athletes underestimate mental training because it doesn't look like effort. It's standard practice in gymnastics, figure skating and Olympic programs.

Encourage athletes to understand what a movement should feel like, not just what it looks like. And the goal isn't a perfect session — it's repeating correct patterns until they run automatically.
