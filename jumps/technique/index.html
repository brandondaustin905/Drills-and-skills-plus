How jumps are scored

In the United Scoring System, jumps sit under the Tumbling category and are judged on two things: difficulty and execution. Jumps receive an execution score out of 2.

Judges take points off using a set of drivers — the specific things they're watching. For execution, those are Approach, Body Control, Landings and Synchronization.

That's the framework. The rest of this page is organised by it.

Flexibility, stretching and programming are in Mobility for Jumps. This lesson is execution only.

The four drivers

Driver 1

Approach

The part before you leave the floor — and the part athletes think about least. Judges see the prep before they see the jump.

Arms below shoulder heightMakes the whole jump look tired even with good legs. → Sharp motion, correct level.
Sloppy or drifting prepThe swing has to be crisp and identical every time. → Same prep, every rep.
Chest dropping on the loadIf the chest goes first, the jump is already compromised. → Hips back, chest proud.
RushingBuying height with speed. → Timing between arm swing and leg lift creates the height. Rushing breaks it, and it shows as rep-to-rep inconsistency.
Driver 2

Body control

Where most points are actually lost. Judges watch head, arm and shoulder placement, hips, leg placement and pointed toes.

Bent kneesThe leg is higher than you can control. → Lower it until they lock, then build back. A lower jump with straight legs beats a high bent one.
Flexed feetPointing at the last second isn't pointing. → From the ankle through the toes, in every drill.
Leaning forwardThe body tips to meet the legs. → Legs come up; chest stays where it is.
Dropped chestMakes a jump look low even when the legs are high. → Fastest fix on this page.
Looking downDrops the chest and takes the head out of line. → Spot forward.
Hips rotating openTurning one hip to get a leg higher reads immediately from the front. → Hips square and level. Lower the leg until they are.
Driver 3

Landings

The part everyone stops paying attention to — and one of four things being scored.

Land tall and finishChest up, arms cleanly back to position, no stumble, no extra step.
Absorb, don't collapseSoft knees, but you're landing into a position, not falling out of one.
Finish the skillA jump ending in a shuffle reads as unfinished. Judges are explicitly looking for controlled, finished landings.
Try thisPractise the landing on its own. Most athletes have never drilled it once, despite it being a quarter of the execution score.
Driver 4

Synchronization

Individually perfect and half a count late still costs the team.

Everyone leaves the floor on the same count, peaks at the same moment, lands together.

The two things that break it: athletes with different natural rhythms rushing or lagging the prep, and athletes with big height differences reaching the peak at different times.

Fix in practicePrep on the count out loud, together, slowly, before adding the jump. Then add music.

Combinations

A combination is connected jumps with no pause between them — a double toe touch, or a toe touch, pike and hurdler all connected.

Get two connected clean before adding a third. Speed comes after the connection does.

Jump to tumbling

Under USASF levels, a jump can connect straight into a tumbling skill — back handspring at Level 3, back tuck at Level 4 and up, standing full at Level 5 and up. Level 4 is the first level to include jump-to-back-tuck.

Important detail: these are scored as standing tumbling, not as jumps. Worth knowing which section of the score sheet you're actually feeding.

Check yoursLevels differ between organisations and change between seasons. Confirm against your own division's current rules.

Diagnose yourself properly

Most athletes are wrong about their own faults.

Film five from the side and five from the front. Side shows chest and lean. Front shows hips, leg height difference and pointed toes.

Watch in slow motion, driver by driver. Approach, body control, landing.

Pick one fault. Not three. Drill it for two weeks with slow, deliberate reps, then film again and compare.

Why oneChasing every fault at once fixes none of them. One fault, two weeks, re-check.

For parents and coaches

Praise the shape, not the height. "That looked really clean" builds a better jump than "get it higher."

Two things visible from the sidelines with no technical knowledge: is the chest staying up, and is the landing quiet and finished? Between them those cover a lot of the score sheet.

On scoring Scoring systems vary between event producers and change season to season. The drivers above come from the United Scoring System as administered at Varsity All Star events. Check the score sheet your own events actually use.
