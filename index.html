Back Handspring Progressions

The full phase-by-phase breakdown — what each stage asks of the body, what usually goes wrong, and the order to build it in.

Prerequisite gate. Before back handspring work begins: a 60-second straight-body wall handstand with straight arms, and a bridge with shoulders stacked over hands held 10–20 seconds. A bridge kickover and back walkover on top of that. If those aren't there, they are the training plan.

The five phases

A back handspring is one continuous motion, but it fails in specific places. Knowing which phase is breaking is most of the fix.

Step 1 — Bridges and shoulder work

Tight shoulders are one of the most common reasons athletes compensate by dumping into the lower back. If there's low back soreness after tumbling, shoulders are worth checking first — alongside a conversation with the coach.

Elevated bridge push-offs

Feet on a panel mat or block. Push into a bridge and shift weight fully over the hands until the shoulders pass the wrists. Hold 5 seconds, then push hard through the shoulders to shift back to the feet.

3 sets of 5. Quality of shoulder extension, not reps.

Step 2 — Handstand snap downs

Target: the fast switch from extended to hollow. This is where block power comes from.

Chest to wall, hands about six inches out. Shrug tall through the shoulders. Snap the legs down by contracting the core, block off the floor through the shoulders, and rebound straight up into a tight pencil jump.

4 sets of 8.

Step 3 — Wedge mat sit and drive

Target: the sit and the backward trajectory, without fear of the fall.

Stand at the high end of a wedge, back facing down the incline. Execute the sit with the knees staying behind the toes, drive the arms back, and let the incline catch the line. A spotter guides the hips over so nothing loads the head.

Rep scheme: until the athlete holds a neutral head position five times running.

Step 4 — Connecting it

Target: rhythm, and converting energy from the previous skill into backward speed.

Power bound to BHS entry. An explosive backward bound, low and long. Land loaded — knees and ankles absorbing, not collapsing and not locked rigid — then straight into the sit with no pause.

3 sets of 5 connections.

When the block is mental

Balking is not disobedience and it's rarely stubbornness. It's usually a body refusing a skill it doesn't have the awareness or the trust to complete.

What works: go back a progression rather than forward. Return to the wedge and the spot. Rebuild on soft surfaces where the consequence is low. A few minutes of visualisation at night. And time.

What doesn't: ultimatums, comparisons to teammates, or "just throw it." Pushing an athlete through a block on a dead floor is how blocks become injuries and injuries become quitting.

Safety protocol

Back handsprings are learned at the gym, with a qualified coach and proper matting. This is not a home skill. The progression runs tramp or tumble track, then barrel with spotting, then floor when the coach approves.

No athlete moves from wedge or spot to a dead floor until they can stick 10 consecutive handstand snap downs with a flat, non-arched spine.

The head stays neutral throughout and is never thrown back.

Lower back, wrist or neck pain is a stop-and-report — not something to work through.
