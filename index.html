Start here — the gate

Before back handspring work begins, an athlete should have:

60-second wall handstand — straight body, straight arms.
Bridge, 10–20 seconds — shoulders stacked over hands.
Bridge kickover and ideally a back walkover.

If those aren't there yet, they are the training plan. Nothing below will work without them.

The five phases

It's one continuous motion, but it fails in specific places. Knowing which phase is breaking is most of the fix.

1
Shoulders

Bridges & shoulder range

Tight shoulders are one of the most common reasons athletes compensate by dumping into the lower back. Low back soreness after tumbling? Check here first — and mention it to the coach.

2
Power

Handstand snap downs

The fast switch from extended to hollow. This is where every bit of your block power comes from.

3
Trajectory

Wedge mat sit & drive

Builds the sit and the backward line without the fear of the fall underneath it.

4
Rhythm

Connecting it

Converting energy from the skill before it into backward speed — the difference between a handspring that stops and one that goes somewhere.

Where the hands go

The correction coaches make most and athletes notice least.

Problem solver

Elbows buckling on contactClosed shoulders, hands turned in. → Thumbs forward, rebuild bridge range.
No height, goes nowhereSquatting down instead of sitting back. → Chair cue, wedge reps.
Lower back sore after tumblingRange borrowed from the spine, often tight shoulders. → Step 1, and tell the coach.
Lands dead, no reboundWeak or late shoulder block. → Snap downs into pencil jumps.
Head thrown backLooking for the floor. → Neutral head, back to wedge and spot.

When the block is mental

Balking isn't disobedience and it's rarely stubbornness. It's usually a body refusing a skill it doesn't yet have the awareness or the trust to finish.

What works

Go back a progression, not forward. Return to the wedge and the spot. Rebuild where the consequence is low. A few minutes of visualisation at night. And time.

What doesn't

Ultimatums, comparisons to teammates, or "just throw it." Pushing an athlete through a block on a dead floor is how blocks become injuries, and injuries become quitting.

Safety protocol

This is a gym skill. Qualified coach, proper matting. Not a driveway, not a backyard trampoline. The progression runs tramp or tumble track → barrel with spotting → floor when the coach says so.

No athlete moves from wedge or spot to a dead floor until they can stick 10 consecutive snap downs with a flat, non-arched spine.

Head stays neutral. Never thrown back.

Lower back, wrist or neck pain is a stop-and-report — never something to work through.
