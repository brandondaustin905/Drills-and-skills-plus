What's actually written in the rulebook, what's good coaching, and why knowing the difference matters at Level 5–7.

How to read this

Rule Written in Cheer Canada's published rulebooks. Not opinion — the actual standard, and enforced.

Practice Sound coaching that isn't a written rule. Useful, but your coach's judgement governs.

Most safety content blurs these together. Separating them tells you what's negotiable and what isn't.

How you're allowed to train

Rule

Surfaces

Athletes must always practise and perform on an appropriate surface. Technical skills — stunts, pyramids, tosses and tumbling — may not be performed on concrete, asphalt, wet or uneven surfaces, or surfaces with obstructions.

Rule

Supervision

All athletes must be supervised during official functions by a qualified director or coach.

Rule

Emergency response plan

All teams, coaches and program directors must have one in place.

Rule

Proficiency gates progression

Coaches must require proficiency before skill progression, and must consider athlete, group and team skill levels when placing athletes at a performance level.

That's the entire progression philosophy, and it's binding. One good landing isn't proficiency.

What you wear

Rule

Jewelry

Jewelry of any kind must be removed and may not be taped over. Only exception: medical ID tags or bracelets, allowed provided they're taped to the body.

Rule

Footwear

Soft-soled shoes with a solid sole must be worn while competing. No dance shoes, boots, or gymnastics slippers.

Rule

Nothing in the mouth

No gum, candy, cough drops or similar during participation. Choking risk.

Rule

Costumes

An athlete in full head or body costume must not spot, stunt, or tumble.

Tumbling legality

The ones athletes at this level actually lose points for.

Rule

Origin and landing

All tumbling must originate from and land on the performing surface.

Rule

No twisting after a back handspring step-out

The feet must come together after the skill before any twist or turn. A back handspring step-out moving straight into a half turn is not allowed.

Rule

Jumps break a pass

Jumps aren't tumbling from a legality standpoint. A jump inside a tumbling pass breaks the pass.

Rule

Nothing in the way

No tumbling over, under, or through a stunt, person, or prop. No tumbling while holding a prop.

Rule

Dive rolls

Allowed — but not in a swan or arched position, and not with any twisting.

Coaching practice at this level

Practice

Warm-up

10–15 minutes of sport-specific dynamic movement before impact work. Active and progressive, not a stretch session.

Practice

Spotting

Track the hips, protect the head and neck, and stay in contact through the whole landing rather than releasing the instant the feet touch.

Practice

Hesitation

Balking mid-skill is a real injury mechanism — the core loosens and the shape fails at the worst moment. Box breathing before a pass; honest confidence check before inverting. Low confidence is a signal to drop back down the progression, not to push through.

Covered in full in the Mental Training & Fear Management lesson, including when a block needs more than a coach.
Practice

Fatigue

As athletes tire, timing and air awareness degrade — exactly when hard skills get dangerous. Quality reps while fresh, real rest between sets, and the honesty to stop when precision drops.

The bottom line

At this level the skills are impressive enough that it's tempting to let standards slide chasing them. Don't.

Approved surfaces. Proficiency before progression. Trained spotting. Fresh reps. The rules exist because people got hurt establishing them.

All of this happens at the gym under a certified coach. Nothing here is a home-training guide.
