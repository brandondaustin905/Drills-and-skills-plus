Why hesitation is the dangerous part

Balking mid-skill isn't just frustrating — it's a genuine injury mechanism.

This is why "just throw it" is bad coaching. A half-committed skill is more dangerous than one not attempted.

Does visualization actually work?

Reasonably well, yes — with one honest caveat.

Imagined and executed movements show strong similarities in timing, in heart and breathing responses, and in which brain structures activate. Researchers describe them as functionally equivalent for training purposes, and motor imagery has measurable benefits for motor learning, rehab after injury, and sports performance.

The caveat: those same researchers are clear that imagined and real movements are distinct. Visualization builds familiarity and confidence. It doesn't replace reps.

One detail worth using: imagining how a movement feels resembles real execution more closely than picturing it from the outside. Feel it, don't just watch it.

Mental rehearsal

Quiet space, eyes closed. Run the whole skill start to finish at real speed — no fast-forwarding through the scary part.

Load it with sensory detail: the feel of the floor on the block, the visual cue at your spot, the sensation of a tight, locked finish.

DoseA few minutes, a few times a week. Consistency beats one long session.

The ladder

Confidence can't be forced. You scale one variable at a time until the brain accepts the new level as safe.

When a block is more than a block

Most blocks resolve with patience and a step backwards. Some don't, and it helps to know the difference.

Worth talking to someone qualified — a sport psychologist, a counsellor, or your doctor — if:

The fear is spreading beyond one skill. There's dread before practice, or trouble sleeping. Your athlete stops wanting to go to the gym. Or it started after a fall or an injury.

None of that means anything is wrong with the athlete. Blocks after a scare are extremely common and they are not a character flaw. But they aren't always something a coach can drill away, and pushing harder in those situations usually makes it worse.

For coaches and parents

Never shame or rush an athlete in a block. Drop them calmly down the ladder until the rhythm comes back. Treat it as a training decision, not a punishment.

Watch the pressure at home. An athlete in a block is usually already frustrated with themselves. Comparisons to teammates, deadlines, and "you had it last week" all raise the stakes at exactly the moment you want them lowered.

Mental blocks are a breakdown in trust, not a physical limitation. Rebuilding trust takes the time it takes.
