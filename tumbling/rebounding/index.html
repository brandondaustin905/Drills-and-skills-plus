Drills & Skills+ · Tumbling 02

Rebounding
& the Set

Height doesn't come from strong legs. It comes from what happens in the half-second your feet are on the floor.

⏱ 7 min read🤸 All levels🏟 Sprung surface only

Why this one is hard

Jumping is natural. Rebounding isn't.

Humans jump — we're built for it. But nothing in ordinary life asks you to take horizontal speed and redirect it straight up. So athletes turn up already decent at jumping and completely untrained at rebounding.

That's why this gets its own lesson, and why the strongest legs in the gym often don't belong to the highest tumbler.

The three phases

The set is decided before you land. By the time your feet touch, everything that determines your height has already been set up.

Phase 1 · Before contact

Arms locked by the ears. Toes pointed down. Core braced. You are arriving already loaded.

Phase 2 · Contact

Hips neutral and tucked under. Body close to vertical, head neutral. The floor compresses under you.

Phase 3 · Launch

Drive through the ankles, violent shrug upward, body long and tight. The floor returns what you gave it.

The half-second that decides everything

Between landing and leaving, one of two things happens.

The cue is pop, not push. And because this is a nervous-system skill rather than a strength one, it improves faster than most athletes expect — as long as the reps stay fresh.

The hollow body rule

Arching the back during a set sends energy backward instead of upward.

1
Foundation

Rebound & stick

Small explosive vertical jump driving through the balls of the feet. Arms shoot straight up and lock by the ears. Land soft and hold it completely frozen.

Golden ruleThe landing must be silent. A stomp means the core isn't braced.
2
Shape

Rebound → tuck → stick

Same rebound. At the absolute peak, drive the knees to the chest into a tight tuck, then snap straight and stick the landing.

Golden ruleKnees to chest, never chest to knees. The upper body stays tall.
3
Speed

Power hurdle rebound

Sharp, aggressive hurdle into a tight two-foot landing. The instant the feet touch, redirect all that forward speed straight up into a maximal vertical rebound.

Golden ruleContact time as short as you can make it. Snap and pop — no settling, no gathering.

Trouble-shooter

Loud, stomping landingsCore isn't braced on contact. → Back to Level 1, silent reps only.
Arms swing up after landingToo late — the set is already gone. → Arms locked by the ears before the feet touch.
Deep knee bend, no heightContact time too long, energy absorbed. → Shorter, stiffer contact. Think pop.
Travelling backward instead of upArching through the set. → Ribs down, body long.
Chest folding to meet the kneesTuck is being made by collapsing. → Knees come up. Chest stays tall.

Safety standards

Surface matters more here than almost anywhere. Spring floors, air tracks, or high-density panel mats only. Never concrete. Never thin, low-density foam.

High-velocity rebounding loads the ankles, calves and Achilles hard. Warm up properly — cold calves plus maximal rebounding is how tendons get hurt.

Keep it to 3–5 quality reps per set, then rest. This is nervous-system work. Tired reps aren't just useless, they're where the injuries happen.

Heel pain in a still-growing athlete is common with repeated impact and is a stop-and-report — never something to push through.
